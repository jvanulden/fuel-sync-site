# FuelSync Brand Book

## 1. Brand summary

FuelSync is a training-connected fueling platform for endurance athletes.

Core idea:
- Training data in.
- Fueling decisions out.

Brand role:
- Translate complexity into confident action.
- Make sports nutrition feel structured, practical, and execution-ready.
- Sit between training intent and race-day reality.

## 2. Positioning

### Category
- Endurance training and sports nutrition software

### Primary audience
- Runners
- Cyclists
- Triathletes
- Endurance athletes preparing for events

### Problem
- Athletes plan training but struggle to operationalize fueling, recovery, and replenishment.

### Promise
- FuelSync turns training load into practical fueling guidance and a buyable replenishment plan.

### Differentiation
- Deterministic and legible instead of opaque
- Practical instead of aspirational-only
- Built around execution, not abstract wellness language
- Structured for real training weeks, not generic meal-planning

## 3. Brand essence

### Brand attributes
- Clear
- Grounded
- Athletic
- Precise
- Calm under pressure
- Credible

### Brand personality
- More coach-operator than lifestyle influencer
- More sharp and prepared than loud and aggressive
- More practical than inspirational

### Emotional outcome
- The athlete feels organized, ready, and ahead of the week.

## 4. Messaging framework

### Core tagline
- Training data in. Fueling decisions out.

### Supporting lines
- Training-connected fueling for endurance athletes.
- Turn the next seven days of workouts into a practical fueling plan.
- Operationalize nutrition before key sessions and race week.
- Fuel the work you already planned.

### Value pillars

#### 1. Connect
- Bring workouts and events into one view.
- Convert training structure into nutrition context.

#### 2. Classify
- Bucket sessions by intensity and duration.
- Make demand explicit and actionable.

#### 3. Execute
- Show pre, during, and post fueling guidance.
- Forecast what the athlete needs to have on hand.

#### 4. Replenish
- Map category demand to a brand catalog.
- Reduce missed sessions caused by poor preparation.

## 5. Voice and tone

### Voice principles
- Use direct language.
- Prefer operational words over vague wellness language.
- Sound informed, not clinical.
- Keep claims disciplined and believable.
- Avoid hype unless it is anchored in a real workflow outcome.

### Tone by context

#### Landing page
- Confident
- Structured
- High signal

#### Product UI
- Clear
- Concise
- Calm

#### Email/reminders
- Timely
- Practical
- Minimal

#### Race-week guidance
- Reassuring
- Specific
- No surprises

### Copy style

Use:
- Fueling brief
- Weekly plan
- Restock signal
- Race-week guidance
- Replenishment
- Workout class
- Key session

Avoid:
- Biohacking
- Peak optimization magic
- AI coach language
- Medical or diagnostic phrasing
- Bro-science supplement claims

## 7. Color system

These values should remain the core palette unless a deliberate rebrand happens.

### Primary palette
- `Ink`: `#172013`
- `Forest`: `#2D5E37`
- `Forest Soft`: `#D9EAD4`
- `Amber`: `#C97A27`
- `Warm Background`: `#F5F0E6`
- `Panel`: `#FFFAF2`
- `Panel Strong`: `#15210F`
- `Muted Text`: `#5D6659`
- `Line`: `rgba(23, 32, 19, 0.12)`

### Functional usage
- Forest: primary action, emphasis, key labels
- Forest Soft: supporting highlights, pills, soft panels
- Amber: roadmap accents, numbered markers, limited highlights
- Ink: primary text and strong headings
- Warm Background / Panel: page surfaces
- Panel Strong: featured cards and high-contrast modules

### Color ratios
- 65% warm neutrals
- 20% deep green / ink
- 10% soft green
- 5% amber

### Accessibility notes
- Use deep green or ink for text over light surfaces.
- Reserve amber for accents, not long text blocks.
- On dark cards, use white text with softened white secondary copy.

## 8. Typography

### Primary display font
- `Space Grotesk`

Use for:
- Hero headlines
- Section titles
- Key metric labels
- Card titles

### Primary body font
- `IBM Plex Sans`

Use for:
- Paragraphs
- Navigation
- Supporting UI copy
- Lists and table text

### Typographic character
- Geometric and modern in headlines
- Neutral and readable in body copy
- Compact, useful, and clean rather than ornamental

### Typographic rules
- Headlines should feel tight and high-confidence.
- Body copy should stay readable and restrained.
- Avoid overly long hero copy blocks.
- Use uppercase eyebrow labels sparingly and consistently.

## 9. Layout principles

### Principles
- Structure first
- Rhythm through panels and spacing
- Clear information hierarchy
- Emphasize preparedness, not decoration

### UI patterns to favor
- Rounded panel containers
- Framed cards with clear hierarchy
- Soft glass or translucent layers only when subtle
- Strong metric blocks
- Planning surfaces over dashboard clutter

### UI patterns to avoid
- Generic startup gradients
- Excessive pills and badges
- Feature grids with no narrative
- Loud dark mode by default
- Clinical medical-app styling

## 10. Imagery

### Imagery direction
- Endurance athletes in context
- Preparation moments over finish-line clichés
- Real training environments
- Gear, bottles, tables, notes, ride/run planning

### Preferred image moments
- Pre-session setup
- Post-session recovery
- Nutrition laid out before a long day
- Quiet race-week preparation

### Avoid
- Hyper-muscular gym imagery
- Generic stock fitness smiling-at-camera shots
- Lab coat / biotech visuals
- Unrealistic supplement hero renders

## 11. Iconography and graphic language

### Icon style
- Simple
- Geometric
- Lightweight but not fragile
- Functional over playful

### Graphic motifs
- Route-like flows
- Stacked cards
- Timelines
- Fueling categories
- Session markers

### Shape language
- Rounded corners
- Soft capsules
- Solid blocks with measured borders

## 12. Motion

### Motion character
- Purposeful
- Minimal
- Sequenced

### Use motion for
- Section reveals
- Card elevation on hover
- Content sequencing
- Progress or timeline emphasis

### Avoid
- Bouncy novelty motion
- Constant pulsing
- Decorative animation without meaning

## 13. Product naming and terminology

### Preferred product nouns
- Weekly plan
- Fueling brief
- Restock signal
- Replenishment cart
- Workout class
- Race-week summary

### Category names
- Electrolytes
- Drink mix
- Gel
- Recovery protein
- Protein
- Recovery support

### Brand naming guidance
- Keep names functional and literal.
- Prefer “weekly plan” over “performance cockpit.”
- Prefer “restock” over “stack.”

## 14. Copy examples

### Good
- Turn the next seven days of workouts into a practical fueling plan.
- Prepare for long sessions before they become logistics problems.
- Map category demand to the products you already trust.

### Bad
- Unlock your inner beast with AI-powered fueling intelligence.
- Bio-optimize every workout with next-generation nutrition science.
- Crush your goals with the smartest supplement engine on earth.

## 15. Homepage application

For the current public site:

### Hero
- Lead with operational clarity
- Keep the headline short and strong
- Show a practical training/fueling panel beside the main message

### Mid-page sections
- Use narrative flow: connect -> classify -> execute -> replenish
- Keep examples concrete
- Favor one strong example over too many abstract claims

### Footer
- Keep it quiet and functional

## 16. Brand guardrails

Never:
- Make medical claims
- Sound like a supplement ad
- Overclaim optimization
- Use AI as the primary value proposition
- Drift into generic wellness language

Always:
- Anchor messaging in training structure
- Keep the athlete’s week in view
- Make the next action obvious
- Show how guidance leads to preparation
